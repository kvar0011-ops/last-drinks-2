Act I · The Scale of It

The Big Picture

One in four Australians
drinks above safe limits

How often do Australians drink?

Nearly 70% of adults drank alcohol in the past year. About 5% drink daily — a pattern associated with long-term health risk. Only 15% have never drunk at all.

The 2022–23 National Drug Strategy Household Survey shows weekly drinking accounts for 36% of the adult population — the largest single group.

Source: AIHW National Drug Strategy Household Survey 2022–23

Litres of pure alcohol per person aged 15+. COVID caused a temporary spike in 2020–21; 2023–24 saw the steepest recorded single-year decline.

Source: AIHW Alcohol Available for Consumption in Australia, 2025

"The 2023–24 drop was the largest single-year decline in per capita alcohol availability since records began in 1960."
Act II · Where Australia Drinks

Geography of Drinking

The NT leads. Victoria trails.
But all states have improved since 2007.

Risky alcohol consumption varies significantly by state and territory. The Northern Territory records the highest rate at 31.1%, while Victoria has the lowest at 23.8%. Crucially, every single state has reduced risky drinking compared to 2007.

Act III · What We Drink

The Changing Glass

Wine overtook beer in 2014.
Spirits are surging. RTDs collapsed.

Stacked area: each band shows one beverage's share of total consumption. Watch wine overtake beer around 2014–15.

Source: AIHW Alcohol Available for Consumption in Australia, 2025

A glass half empty for beer

Beer dominated Australian drinking for most of the 20th century. But since 2014–15, wine has been the biggest contributor to total alcohol consumed per capita.

Spirits have surged since 2016, driven by craft gin, premium whisky, and premix cocktails. RTDs collapsed after a tax increase in 2008.

The slope chart below shows who won and who lost between 2005 and 2023 in clear, direct comparison.

Slope chart: rising lines = gained share. Beer fell sharply; wine and spirits rose.

Act V · The Cost

Human & Economic Cost

$72.9 billion a year.
Alcohol is Australia's most expensive habit.

A staggering social burden

The estimated social cost of alcohol in Australia was $72.9 billion in 2020–21, projected to exceed $75 billion by 2022–23.

The biggest costs are not pubs — they are lives cut short prematurely (39.9%) and years lost to illness (31.9%). Road crashes, crime, and workplace losses make up the remainder.

Custom treemap — cell size proportional to cost. Premature mortality and morbidity account for over 70% of total social cost.

Source: Gadsden et al. 2024 / AIHW 2025

Total alcohol-related hospital admissions per year. COVID-19 caused a spike in 2020–21 with 88,420 admissions.

Source: AIHW National Hospital Morbidity Database 2023–24

Alcohol has been the #1 reason Australians seek drug treatment for a decade — 42% of all episodes in 2023.

Source: AIHW AODTS Annual Report 2023–24

Key insight: Almost 3 in 5 drug-related hospitalisations in 2023–24 involved alcohol — more than cannabis, methamphetamine, and heroin combined. Alcohol is by far Australia's most harmful legal substance.

"Alcohol is Australia's most costly legal substance — but consumption is finally falling."

Act VI · The Long View

Trends Over Time

Two decades of data —
Australia is slowly sobering up

% consuming alcohol at risky levels over two decades. Males consistently higher; both sexes show the long-term declining trend with a notable spike in the 2022 survey.

Source: AIHW NDSHS multiple waves 2001–2022–23

Australia is drinking less

Per capita consumption peaked in 2007–08 at 11.4 litres and has trended downward since. The 2023–24 figure of 9.8 litres is the lowest in decades.

Among 18–24 year-olds, the shift is most pronounced. Drinking less is becoming the cultural norm, driven by cost-of-living pressure, growing health awareness, and a boom in alcohol-free alternatives.

"Among 18–24 year-olds, the cultural attitude toward alcohol has shifted. Drinking less is no longer unusual — it's becoming the norm."
